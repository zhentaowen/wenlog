2026.05.07

It’s been over a year and a half since I last wrote a blog. Life still feels like walking on thin ice—every step has to be careful, or I might fall.

Recently, I lost a long-time client I’d worked with for three years. It really hurt. Now I’m trying to find remote jobs again, and also looking for full-time positions locally—but the chances are almost none, and the competition is fierce.

To survive, I’ve even thought about doing temporary jobs like delivering milk or food. I heard there are shady practices behind some milk delivery companies—middlemen keep cutting prices, making it hard for workers. I don’t have a motorcycle or an electric scooter. What if I tried delivering food on a bicycle through Meituan’s platform? It would be tough, but at least it’d be more honest than getting ripped off by multiple layers of middlemen. I’ve also been thinking about setting up a small street stall selling coffee or pizza—just to try my luck. Even if I only make a few dozen yuan a day, it’s better than just waiting around hoping for something to happen.

At the end of last month, I finally finished the first draft of a book cover design project. To build up experience, I also took on two free trial projects on my own. Sometimes, doing free work when you’re unemployed can actually be an opportunity. But one client said he liked it at first—then kept asking for changes again and again. After four rounds of edits, I just couldn’t take it anymore because clearly we weren’t on the same page about what he wanted. If paid fairly, this job should have cost two or three times what he offered—but how do you say that? So I just bit my tongue and finished it anyway.

The other project was the same—five rounds of revisions—and then suddenly he stopped replying.

Today was the first working day after the Labor Day holiday. Xena went to work and Sam started school again. After dinner, the living room went quiet all of a sudden—and when I looked up, it was already past midnight! I had planned to hang out with them for a bit, watch something together or play games—but time slipped away without me even noticing.

I realized how often I just stay stuck in that one old work chair. That chair feels like a time machine: once you sit down, time flies by so fast. By the time you stand up again, everything has changed—the kids are older now, friends have moved away, chances have passed… but here I am still stuck in place.
