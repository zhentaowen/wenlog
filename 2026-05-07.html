2026.05.07

It’s been a year and a half since my last blog post. Life still feels like walking on thin ice; I am in a position where I simply cannot afford to make mistakes.

A few months ago, I lost a steady client I had been working with for three years. It hurt deeply. Lately, I've been trying hard to find remote work, but the opportunities are scarce and the competition is fierce. I’ve even started looking for local full-time jobs, but those doors seem closed right now, too.

To make ends meet and keep my family afloat, I’ve been considering picking up some gig work. I looked into delivering milk, but I heard there are a lot of shady practices—middlemen taking huge cuts—which worries me. Since I don’t own a scooter or a motorcycle, I thought maybe I could try delivering food for Meituan on my bicycle. It would be grueling physical work, but at least it's better than being exploited by multiple layers of middlemen. I’ve also been thinking about setting up a small street stall selling coffee or pizza. Even if I only make a few dozen bucks a day, it’s far better than sitting around waiting for luck to strike.

On the design front, I took on a book cover project late last month and finally finished the first draft today. To build my portfolio during downtimes, I also agreed to do two unpaid trial projects. Honestly, doing free trials can be a decent way to gain experience when you’re out of work.

However, it's been a rough ride. One client loved my trial and hired me at my standard rate, but then came the endless demands for revisions. After four rounds of changes, I reached my breaking point—our visions were completely misaligned. If I were to start over with them now, I would have to charge two or three times my rate to justify this level of back-and-forth. But asking for more money halfway through felt unprofessional, so I just bit the bullet and finished the job. The second trial project was even worse: after five rounds of free revisions, the client just completely ghosted me.

Lesson learned the hard way: From now on, I am writing a strict revision limit into my contracts. It’s not just about protecting my time; it’s about maintaining the quality of the project and my sanity.

Today was the first workday after the May Day holiday. This morning, Zena went to work, and Sam went back to school. In the blink of an eye, it was evening. After they came home and we had dinner, the living room suddenly fell dead silent. I looked up and realized how late it had gotten. I had originally planned to watch a video or play a game with them, but the time had just slipped away without me noticing.

I realized that I am always just sitting here, trapped in this office chair. This chair feels exactly like a time machine: the moment you sit down, time flies forward at warp speed. By the time you finally stand up, the world around you has changed—your kids have grown, your friends have moved out of your life, opportunities have slipped by... and yet, you are still spinning in the exact same place.
